Generally artist pastels come in two main forms: Soft / Hard Pastels, and Oil Pastels. Soft pastels are a mix of pigment, chalk, gum tragacanth, and alcohol to dissolve the pigment.

Oil pastels, while technically called pastels, are considered a different medium: these are generally Pigment, Linseed Oil, and Beeswax.

My Own Recipe

This recipe is for an experimental pastel that acts more like an ink stick than a pigment stick, with aspects similar to both Soft Pastels and Oil Pastels. These could be considered a kind of watercolor stick, except that I use ink:

2/3 Cup Of Chalk
1 Cup Of Charcoal or Graphite
Emulsion of Isopropyl, Rice Vinegar, and Olive Oil / Peanut Oil
1 tablespoon Gum Tragacanth
Hand Spatula

Optional

Egg Yolks ( for creating Gouache Sticks )

Process

Mix in a bowl the the chalk with the charcoal or graphite, and add a little bit at a time up to around 2/3 Cup of alcohol, rice vinegar, and olive oil. You can substitute Olive Oil for Peanut Oil. But the emphasis is on a drying oil.

Add 1 tablespoon of Gum tragacanth. Continue mixing until it forms a thick wet paste, until it becomes firm like pie dough. Remove from bowl and place on either a marbled surface or stove top.

Carefully spread around the dough on the stove top to make sure pigment ink, binder, and chalk is evenly mixed with the emulsion so that there is as little powder as possible. The longer you do this the better the quality of pastel. This can take between 5 to twenty minutes depending on how well mixed.

One difference with my approach is these should be "Shadow Dried" rather than dried out in the sun, in order to make the drying last as long as possible.

Particularities With Activated Charcoal

Sometimes pastels made out of activated charcoal with olive oil will develop a superficial protective film similarly to oil pastels. Just use the same utility knife you use to sharpen Reed Pen for bamboo calligraphy.

Sometimes these will also develop an odor that can be described as soaplike. I^m still currently investigating why this is the case.

Further Exploration

I've also tweaked this recipe to work with using Sumi Ink, although it develops a firmer consistency than standard ink based pastels. Possibly do to the presence of Animal glue that holds Sumi Ink together.
